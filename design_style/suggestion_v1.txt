Yes. Your current design has a strong “academic broadsheet” identity, but for
  easy to read + low stress, I’d soften it. Right now the beige paper texture,
  double-line masthead, crimson accents, dense cards, and animated/outlined
  chips make it feel a little high-stakes. For grant search, users are already
  anxious, so the UI should feel quieter.

  Main Direction
  Move from editorial newspaper to calm research workspace.

  I’d keep:

  - serif logo / brand character
  - teal as the primary action color
  - clear eligibility chips
  - card-based results

  I’d change:

  - reduce the parchment/newspaper feeling
  - reduce crimson usage
  - make backgrounds flatter
  - make cards less visually busy
  - make status colors calmer

  Color Suggestions
  Current palette:

  - beige paper background
  - dark ink
  - teal
  - crimson
  - amber
  - green

  I’d shift to:

  --paper: #f7f8f5;
  --paper-2: #fbfcfa;
  --card: #ffffff;
  --ink: #20231f;
  --ink-soft: #5c6258;
  --ink-faint: #8a9185;

  --line-strong: #cbd4c5;

  --teal: #2f766d;

  --crimson: #a44a5b;
  --crimson-wash: #f8ecef;
  --amber: #9a6a2f;
  --amber-wash: #f7f0e3;
  --green: #557a4b;
  --green-wash: #eef5ea;

  This keeps personality but lowers contrast stress.

  Most Important Visual Changes

  1. Remove or reduce the dotted paper texture
     The radial background pattern adds visual noise. For low-stress reading,
     use a flat background or extremely subtle texture.

     Current:

     background-image: radial-gradient(...);

     I’d remove it or make it almost invisible.

  2. Reduce crimson prominence
     Crimson is useful for true blockers, but if it appears too often, the page
     feels like it is warning the user constantly. Use crimson only for hard
     ineligibility. Use amber/gray for “verify” or “future target.”

  3. Make cards calmer
     Current cards have strong borders, shadows, animation, badges, tags, chips,
     meters, and buttons all competing. I’d:
      - reduce shadows
      - reduce hover lift
      - use lighter borders
      - make the score meter smaller or less saturated
      - put eligibility chips on their own line

  4. Rename and soften alarming labels
     Instead of:
      - blocked
      - ineligible
      - past window

     Use:
      - Not currently eligible
      - Future target
      - Verify timing
      - Needs PR/citizenship

     This is lower stress while still honest.

  5. Make the top masthead less formal
     The double black border makes it feel official/legal. I’d replace:

     border-bottom: 3px double var(--ink);

     with:

     border-bottom: 1px solid var(--line);

  6. Use “quiet dashboard” spacing
     The dashboard is information-dense, which is good, but chips and badges
     need more hierarchy. I’d make the result card order:
      - Title
      - Funder + source
      - Fit reason
      - Award / deadline / stage
      - Eligibility status
      - Description
      - Actions

     Right now eligibility chips sit near the funder line, which can make the
     top of each card feel crowded.

  Theme Recommendation
  I’d describe the design target as:

  > Calm, clinical, research-office dashboard. Not startup flashy, not
  > government portal, not academic newspaper.

  Visual references:

  - NIH / research admin calmness
  - Notion-like readability
  - Linear-style restraint
  - soft biomedical color palette

  Typography
  Your IBM Plex Sans choice is good. Fraunces gives personality, but I’d use it
  mostly for the brand and major section headings. Keep result titles readable,
  not too decorative. Scientists scanning grants need clarity more than
  editorial flair.

  One Specific UI Suggestion
  Add a low-stress summary panel above results:

  You have 14 grants open now
  9 future targets
  3 need citizenship / PR
  Sorted by Claude fit

  Use soft green / gray / amber, not red-heavy. This gives control and reduces
  anxiety.

  My Strongest Recommendation
  Keep the app beautiful, but make it quieter. Your product’s value is trust and
  clarity. The best design should feel like:

  > “Here is what is worth your time, here is what is not, and nothing is being
  > hidden.”

  That means softer colors, less visual noise, fewer red alerts, and more
  breathing room around the key decisions.
